‚è∞ What is a Unix Timestamp?

A Unix timestamp (also known as Epoch time or POSIX time) represents the number of seconds that have elapsed since January 1, 1970 00:00:00 UTC‚Äîa moment called the Unix Epoch. It's a single integer that increases by one every second.

Because it's just a number, it's incredibly efficient for computers to store, sort, and calculate dates, regardless of timezones. When you convert 1735689600 to a human-readable date, your device applies your local timezone offset to show you the correct local time.

Why developers use Unix timestamps:

  • Timezone Independence: The same timestamp means the same instant worldwide
  • Easy Calculations: Add 86400 to get tomorrow, subtract 3600 for an hour ago
  • Storage Efficiency: Just 4-8 bytes for any date in history or future
  • Sorting: Numeric comparison is faster than parsing date strings
  • API Compatibility: The universal format most APIs expect and return

‚ö†Ô∏è Common Gotchas

Seconds vs. Milliseconds

The most common timestamp bug: mixing up seconds (10 digits) and milliseconds (13 digits).

  • Python, PHP, Ruby, SQL: Use seconds (e.g., 1736140800)
  • JavaScript, Java: Use milliseconds (e.g., 1736140800000)

Symptom: If your date shows 1970 (or year 54000+), you likely need to multiply or divide by 1000.

The Year 2038 Problem

32-bit signed integers max out at 2,147,483,647. Unix time will hit this on January 19, 2038 at 03:14:07 UTC. One second later, 32-bit systems will wrap to December 13, 1901. Most modern systems use 64-bit integers, which won't overflow for 292 billion years.

Daylight Saving Time

DST creates two types of problems: hours that happen twice (when clocks fall back) and hours that don't exist (when clocks spring forward). Always store in UTC to avoid these issues‚Äîconvert to local time only for display.

Leap Seconds

Unix time ignores leap seconds by definition. A day is always exactly 86,400 seconds. Real-world clocks occasionally add a 61st second to stay synchronized with Earth's rotation, but Unix timestamps just keep counting linearly.

‚ùì Frequently Asked Questions

Why January 1, 1970?

Historical convention. Unix was being developed in the early 1970s, and the designers needed a reference point. 1970 was recent, round, and practical for the systems of that era. It stuck.

Can timestamps be negative?

Yes. Negative Unix timestamps represent dates before 1970. For example, -86400 is December 31, 1969. Most modern systems handle this correctly.

Why use UTC instead of local time?

Consistency. UTC never changes‚Äîno DST, no political timezone adjustments. Store everything in UTC, convert to local time only when displaying to users. This prevents countless bugs.

How do I handle timezone conversions in code?

Use libraries. JavaScript has Intl.DateTimeFormat. Python has pytz or zoneinfo. Never try to manually add/subtract hour offsets‚ÄîDST rules are complex and change frequently.

What's ISO 8601?

The international standard for date/time strings. Format: 2026-01-23T14:30:00Z. The "T" separates date and time, "Z" means UTC. Use this when you need a human-readable string that's also unambiguous and sortable.
